Cross-Family Knowledge Distillation in Interpretable Knowledge Space

Transferring knowledge from a stronger teacher to a weaker student of a different model family by aligning them in sparse-autoencoder knowledge space — rather than in raw-activation or logit space.

Teacher Llama-3.1-8B (llama, d=4096) Student Qwen3-0.6B (qwen, d=1024) Mechanism TopK SAE knowledge alignment Primary baseline Transport-and-Merge (arXiv 2602.05495) Hardware 2× NVIDIA RTX A6000 Status scaffolded, smoke test 23/23

1.Abstract

know_trans investigates whether knowledge can be distilled from a stronger teacher to a weaker student of a different family by aligning the two models in an interpretable knowledge space — the feature basis of sparse autoencoders (SAEs) — instead of the conventional spaces of raw hidden states or output logits. The central premise is that knowledge items learned by an SAE are a more canonical, monosemantic basis than family-specific activation geometry, so we can transfer knowledge items from teacher to student without forcing the student to copy the teacher's coordinate system.

We train two independent TopK SAEs (one per model) and align their features post-hoc through a shared, externally-defined labeled knowledge battery: each feature is scored as a detector for human-labeled knowledge items via ROC-AUC, and features are matched by shared label rather than by activation correlation. This anchoring deliberately breaks the circularity trap and is robust to the fact that the two families use different tokenizers. The distillation objective then projects both models into a shared knowledge-activation space whose dimension equals the number of shared knowledge items, sidestepping both the hidden-dimension mismatch (4096 vs 1024) and the tokenizer mismatch in a single move.

The falsifiable headline claim is that SAE-based distillation beats hidden-state distillation across families, and the gap widens with family distance. The primary baseline to beat is Transport-and-Merge (optimal transport over raw neurons + weight merge, arXiv 2602.05495); secondary baselines are learned-projector hidden-state distillation and logit distillation. Before any distillation, three validation gates — cross-seed stability, a shuffled-pair null control, and causal steering — must pass. Teacher-only knowledge items (those with no student match) are treated as a headline finding, not noise.

2.Problem Statement

We want strong→weak, cross-family knowledge transfer. The teacher is a larger, more capable model; the student is smaller and from a different lineage. The concrete default pair fixes the difficulty:

RoleModelFamilyd_modelLayersPath
teacherLlama-3.1-8Bllama409632/share1/zhlu6105/models/Llama-3.1-8B
studentQwen3-0.6Bqwen102428/share1/zhlu6105/models/Qwen3-0.6B

This pairing concentrates three obstacles that conventional distillation handles poorly:

  • Hidden-dimension mismatch. 4096 vs 1024 means there is no natural one-to-one correspondence between activation coordinates.
  • Tokenizer mismatch. The two families tokenize text differently, so token-index alignment is impossible. The spec is emphatic: never align by token index. Pool to example/span level.
  • Geometry mismatch. Even at equal width, each family encodes knowledge in its own rotated, superposed coordinate frame; matching raw activations would force the student to reproduce the teacher's geometry.

Two engineering rules follow and are enforced throughout: dimensions are never hardcoded (d_in is inferred from activation shards and d_hidden = expansion × d_in), and layer correspondence is discovered per knowledge item rather than assumed (the best (t_layer, s_layer) pair is selected in scoring/matching).

3.The Reading A vs B Decision

There are two ways to use SAEs in this project, and the choice is foundational.

Reading B (rejected)The SAE is a microscope — an interpretability instrument used only to observe and describe what each model has learned. Under Reading B the SAE never participates in training.
Reading A (chosen)The SAE is the mechanism of distillation. SAE knowledge alignment is used as a training signal: we align teacher and student in knowledge space and that alignment is the loss the student is trained against.

We chose Reading A. The cross-family knowledge-correspondence premise is ours to prove — we are not assuming that interpretable features automatically transfer; we are building the apparatus that lets us test, and then exploit, that correspondence as a distillation objective. Interpretability and steerability remain a secondary payoff, but they are not the goal.

4.The Disentanglement Bet

Why align in SAE knowledge space at all, rather than in the spaces every distillation method already uses?

4.1 Why raw hidden states are the wrong space

Raw hidden states encode knowledge in superposition inside family-specific geometry. A neuron is polysemantic; a direction means something only relative to the rest of that model's basis. Matching raw activations therefore forces the student to copy the teacher's coordinate system — wasteful at best, and possibly impossible across families with different widths and rotations.

4.2 The bet

The disentanglement betSAE knowledge space is a more canonical, monosemantic basis. If knowledge items are disentangled into separate features, we can transfer knowledge items between models without copying geometry — the student need only learn to fire its own knowledge-item-c feature when the teacher fires its knowledge-item-c feature, regardless of how each model internally represents it.

4.3 Why SAE beats the alternatives

ApproachUnit of transferCross-family problem
SAE knowledge distillation (ours)Disentangled knowledge itemsSidestepped: align in knowledge space, geometry is never copied
Hidden-state distillation (learned projector)Raw activationsMust learn a projector that copies superposed, family-specific geometry
Logit distillationOutput distributionTokenizer mismatch corrupts the target vocabulary alignment; no internal knowledge signal
Transport-and-Merge (OT, arXiv 2602.05495)Polysemantic neuronsAligns/merges neurons, not knowledge items; correspondence is implicit and unproven
Falsifiable headlineSAE-distill beats hidden-state-distill across families, and the gap widens with family distance. This is the experiment that confirms or refutes the bet.
Honest riskSAE reconstruction is lossy and sparse. The sparse code may discard exactly the signal that distillation needs — the disentangled basis could be too narrow a channel. This risk is monitored via reconstruction quality, L0, and dead-feature tracking, and is the main reason a "frozen vs jointly-trained student SAE" decision remains open (§14).

5.Correspondence Method: Independent SAEs vs Crosscoder

To establish knowledge correspondence between the two models, there are two architectures. A crosscoder trains a single SAE that reads both models' activations jointly, baking correspondence into the architecture. We instead chose two independent SAEs plus post-hoc matching.

5.1 Why independent SAEs

Independence buys flexibility: each SAE is trained on its own model with its own dimensionality and seeds, the teacher SAE can be trained with multiple seeds for stability analysis, and matching becomes an explicit, inspectable step rather than an architectural assumption. The cost is that we must control three hazards that a crosscoder would (partly) hide.

5.2 The three hazards

#HazardWhat goes wrongControl
aCircularity trapIf matches are derived from the same quantity later minimized by the loss, validation is vacuous — you confirm what you assumed.Anchor matches to an external knowledge label (the battery), which is not the quantity later minimized (§6).
bMatch instabilityDifferent SAE seeds may yield different features, so matches may be artifacts of one random run.Cross-seed Jaccard stability gate over ≥2 teacher seeds (§11.1).
cMany-to-many / partial coverageFeature splitting means a knowledge item maps to a set of features; some teacher knowledge items have no student counterpart.Knowledge→feature sets (not single features); teacher-only knowledge items reported as headline findings, not dropped (§6, §11).

6.The Labeled Knowledge Battery

The matching signal is a labeled knowledge battery: a collection of knowledge items, each defined by human-meaningful positives and hard negatives drawn from external benchmarks. Each model's SAE features are anchored to these knowledge items by detector AUC, and features across models are matched by shared label. We call these knowledge items; the code module and identifiers retain the name concept for now (e.g. concepts.py, the Concept class, concept_activations, data/concepts/).

Why this designIt breaks circularity (the matches are anchored to external labels, not to the activation quantity the loss later minimizes); it is tokenizer-proof (knowledge items are example-level, never token-aligned); and it yields per-item interpretable results. The trade-off: coverage is limited to the battery, to be complemented later by auto-interp.

6.2 Worked safety example — and how it avoids circularity

Consider the knowledge item "harmful request":

  • Positives: AdvBench / HarmBench behaviors.
  • Negatives: benign instructions (e.g. benign MMLU questions).

For each model we run positives and negatives through it, take encode_dense codes pooled to example level, and score every SAE feature as a detector via ROC-AUC. The result for this knowledge item:

ModelFeatureDetector AUCMatched by
teacher Llama-3.1-8Bfeature #48210.97shared label ("harmful request") — not activation correlation
student Qwen3-0.6Bfeature #11900.93

Teacher feature #4821 ↔ student feature #1190, matched because both are strong detectors of the same externally-labeled knowledge item.

Why this avoids the circularity trapThe match between #4821 and #1190 is established through the external knowledge label, by computing each feature's AUC against human-defined positives/negatives. It is not derived from correlating the two models' activations. Because the loss later minimized (knowledge-activation-space MSE, §10) is a different quantity from the AUC-based matching signal, confirming alignment is not circular: we did not assume the correspondence we are trying to demonstrate.

7.4 Why hard negatives

Hard negativesEvery item's negatives are hard: drawn from the same distribution and surface form as the positives but lacking the target knowledge. This forces a feature's detector AUC to mean "detects this knowledge" rather than picking up a surface confound (length, formatting, source domain). With easy negatives a feature could score a high AUC just by recognizing, say, "this is an MMLU question" or "this is an instruction" — telling us nothing about the knowledge we labeled.

Two negative regimes appear, by design:

  • safety uses benign negatives — harmful behaviors vs benign MMLU questions. The contrast is harm vs not.
  • safety_semantic uses other-category harmful negatives — positives from one HarmBench SemanticCategory, negatives from other harmful categories. The contrast is harm type, not harm-vs-benign, so the feature must distinguish which kind of harm rather than merely detecting harm at all.
  • topic_* negatives are questions from other MMLU subjects (same question format), and language_* negatives are the same parallel questions rendered in other languages — so the feature must key on language, not content.

10.The Knowledge-Activation-Space Loss

The key idea of the distillation objective is to map each model into a shared knowledge-activation space whose dimension is the number of shared knowledge items, C. This single move sidesteps both the d_hidden mismatch and the tokenizer mismatch.

10.1 The knowledge-activation space

teacher d_hidden_T student d_hidden_S shared C-dim knowledge space
Figure 2. Knowledge-activation-space alignment. Each model's high-dimensional SAE codes (d_hidden_T ≠ d_hidden_S) are projected into the same C-dimensional knowledge space by pooling over that model's own per-item feature set. Alignment happens in knowledge space, so neither d_hidden nor the tokenizer ever need to match.

10.2 The math (rendered as styled HTML)

For a given example, a model's activation in knowledge dimension c is the mean of that model's encode_dense code over the feature set assigned to knowledge item c:

concept_actc(x)  =  1|Fc| ∑f ∈ Fc   encode_dense(x)f    Fc = model's feature set for knowledge item c

Stacking over the C shared knowledge items gives a vector in knowledge space, computed independently for teacher and student:

aT(x) = [ concept_act1T, …, concept_actCT ] ∈ ℝC   teacher (frozen) aS(x) = [ concept_act1S, …, concept_actCS ] ∈ ℝC   student (trained)

The knowledge-distillation loss aligns the two in knowledge space (MSE or smooth-L1, optionally per-item normalized), and the total objective adds it to the student's language-model loss:

Lknowledge = 1|B| · C ∑x ∈ B   ∥ aS(x) − aT(x) ∥2   (MSE / smooth-L1) Ltotal = Llm + λknowledge · Lknowledge   λknowledge = 1.0 (default); teacher frozen, both SAEs frozen by cfg; grad flows student only

This corresponds directly to the API: concept_activations(codes_dense, concept_sets) → [E, C] produces each model's knowledge vector; concept_distill_loss(student_concept_acts, teacher_concept_acts) computes Lknowledge; and MatchedFeatureDistiller.compute_loss(batch) returns {loss, lm_loss, concept_loss} with the teacher and both SAEs frozen so gradients flow into the student only.

11.The Three Validation Gates

No distillation is run until all three gates pass. They directly defend against the hazards of §5.

11.1 Gate 1 Cross-seed stability (Jaccard)

Train ≥2 teacher SAE seeds and compare their knowledge→feature sets. stability_score(setsA, setsB) → dict[str,float] reports a per-item Jaccard overlap of matched features across seeds. Low Jaccard means a match is a seed artifact (hazard b). Only stable knowledge items proceed.

11.2 Gate 2 Shuffled-pair null control

null_control(teacher_sets, student_sets, n_shuffle=100) → dict shuffles the teacher↔student pairing many times and measures the gap between true and shuffled matches. A real correspondence must score well above the shuffled null; otherwise apparent matches are chance.

11.3 Gate 3 Causal steering

causal_steer_test(teacher, student, t_sae, s_sae, match, prompts) → dict ablates/steers a matched feature via its decoder direction and measures the behavioral delta. The spec is explicit: this must actually run — correlation in detector AUC is not enough; the matched feature must be causally the same knowledge item in both models.

Headline, not noiseTeacher-only knowledge items (no student match, shared:false) are logged separately and treated as a headline finding: they map exactly the capabilities the smaller student lacks.

  • Jaccard stability across SAE seeds (gate 1).

  • Causal behavioral delta from steering matched features (gate 3).
  • Distillation losses: loss, lm_loss, concept_loss.

  • Coverage: shared vs teacher-only knowledge-item counts.

12.3 Headline ablation — vary family distance

The decisive ablationVary family distance between teacher and student and measure the SAE-distill vs hidden-state-distill gap. The bet predicts the gap widens with family distance; same-family pairs should show a smaller advantage than cross-family pairs. Which exact teacher/student pairs realize the distance sweep is an open decision (§14).

13.Related Work

  • Transport-and-Merge (arXiv 2602.05495) — the anchor and primary baseline. It aligns polysemantic neurons via optimal transport and merges weights without training. Its downstream gains are weak evidence of true knowledge-level correspondence; we make knowledge items the unit of correspondence and use them for distillation/SFT rather than merging. The correspondence premise is ours to prove.
  • Sparse autoencoders for interpretability — we adopt the OpenAI-style TopK SAE (fixed sparsity via top-k selection, AuxK dead-feature revival, unit-norm decoder columns). We repurpose SAEs from microscope (Reading B) to distillation mechanism (Reading A).
  • Crosscoders — a joint SAE over both models that bakes in correspondence. We deliberately chose independent SAEs + post-hoc label-anchored matching for flexibility and inspectable correspondence, accepting the three hazards.
  • Knowledge distillation — classical hidden-state and logit distillation are our secondary baselines; both struggle cross-family with the dimension/tokenizer mismatches we sidestep.
  • Safety benchmarks — AdvBench and HarmBench supply the safety knowledge positives; MMLU supplies topic knowledge and benign negatives; Global-MMLU-Lite supplies language knowledge.

How AUC is measured

Each SAE latent's example-pooled activation is treated as a one-dimensional classifier score; we compute the ROC-AUC of that single latent separating the knowledge item's 200 positives from its 200 hard negatives. We report the best latent's AUC and the layer on which it lives.

What it means0.5 = chance; 1.0 = a clean, near-monosemantic detector for that knowledge item. language_hu at 1.000 in both models is a perfect detector; safety at 0.958/0.890 is a strong one. Teacher ≥ student almost everywhere — consistent with the bigger model carrying cleaner, more disentangled features.
CaveatReporting the best of many latents is slightly optimistic (it is a max over thousands of candidates). But the bias is applied identically to teacher and student, so the teacher-vs-student comparison remains fair even if the absolute numbers are inflated.

How "best layer" is set

Discovered, never hardcodedThe "best layer" is the layer whose latents detect the knowledge item best — it is discovered from the data, not assumed. The teacher lands mid-to-late (L16 / L24); the student varies (L7 / L14 / L21). Crucially, the layer correspondence between the two models falls out of the data rather than being imposed — exactly the §2 rule that layer correspondence is discovered per knowledge item.

How "shared" is set

A knowledge item is shared when both models have at least one latent with AUC ≥ τ (0.80). It is teacher-only when only the teacher clears τ, and neither when both miss it.

Caveat — the 0.80 cutoff is fragileThe hard threshold creates threshold artifacts, not firm conclusions:
  • topic_math is labelled teacher-only only because the student's 0.755 just misses 0.80 — a 0.045 gap, not a real absence of the feature.
  • topic_economics is neither because both models sit at ≈0.75 — near, but under, the line.
These calls would flip under a small threshold change. The fix (Takeaway 3) is a softened rank/margin criterion rather than a hard 0.80 cutoff.

Stability asks: if we retrain the teacher SAE with a different random seed, does the same knowledge re-emerge? Run 1 measured this two ways — an old index-based method that failed, and a corrected pattern-based method. The contrast is itself a key methodological finding.

Why the index-Jaccard method FAILED

The predicted hazard, observedThe old metric computed the Jaccard overlap of the (layer, latent-index) sets between SAE seed 0 and seed 1. This is ≈0 by construction: two independently-trained SAEs learn their dictionaries in a different, permuted order, so latent #4821 in seed 0 has nothing to do with #4821 in seed 1 — index overlap is near zero even when the knowledge is represented identically. (The lone 1.0 for topic_economics is the degenerate empty∩empty case: neither seed had a detector ≥ 0.80, so two empty sets "overlap" perfectly.) In short, index-Jaccard measures index identity, not knowledge stability. This is precisely the predicted hazard of the independent-SAE design (§5, hazard b) showing up in real data.

How the pattern-correlation FIX works

The fix compares latents by their activation pattern, which is index-invariant. For each seed-0 detector latent of a knowledge item, take its activation vector over the 40k-token probe set and find the maximum Pearson correlation to any seed-1 latent's activation vector. A value near 1.0 means a latent with the same firing pattern re-emerges under a new seed — i.e. the representation is seed-robust. We report the mean over an item's detector latents, plus each detector's individual value.

What the numbers meanlanguage_hu 1.000 = perfectly universal across seeds; topic_medical 0.835, topic_math 0.769, safety 0.699 = strongly stable. Overall mean 0.826 vs the old artifactual ≈0.2.
The per-detector insightThe breakdown is the real takeaway: each item's primary detector is highly stable (0.82–1.0) while its secondary detectors are flakier (0.58–0.67). The practical rule: weight by the top detector rather than averaging all of them equally.

18.5 Pilot takeaways

Pilot findings — validate the method, not final results
  1. The full pipeline runs end-to-end on real data (capture → SAE → score → match → gates) and the detection AUCs are sane.
  2. Key result: cross-seed stability must be measured by activation pattern, not latent index; once corrected, the teacher's knowledge is genuinely stable (mean 0.83), vindicating the independent-SAE path — no crosscoder pivot needed.
  3. The shared / teacher-only calls need a softened threshold (rank / margin), not a hard 0.80 cutoff — topic_math and topic_economics are threshold artifacts.

19.Measurement Methods — Exact Formulas

A precise, self-contained reference for how every number in this report is calculated. Formulas are rendered as styled HTML/Unicode (no external typesetting). One subsection per metric.

19.1 Example pooling

Every detector score starts from a single per-example activation per latent. For example x and SAE latent f:

af(x)  =  mean over x's non-padding tokens of   encode_dense(token)f   f's dense activation, example-pooled

Tokens are aligned to examples via the capture index (char-span / example_id in index.parquet), so pooling is tokenizer-agnostic — required because teacher and student use different tokenizers and can never be aligned by token index.

19.2 Detector AUC

For knowledge item C with positive set P and hard-negative set Nneg, latent f at layer L:

AUC(f, C, L) = ROC-AUC of { af(x) : x ∈ P } (label 1)  vs  { af(x) : x ∈ Nneg } (label 0) = P( a random positive scores above a random negative ) — 0.5 = chance, 1.0 = perfect AUCC(L) = maxf AUC(f, C, L)   per-layer best best layer = argmaxL AUCC(L)   DISCOVERED, not fixed

19.3 Detector set (concept_feature_sets)

The detector set for knowledge item C is the latents with AUC ≥ τ on the best layer, taken top-k (k ≤ 10) by AUC, with τ = 0.70 (softened).

19.4 Shared / class

Let t = teacher best AUC and s = student best AUC for knowledge item C:

shared  if  min(t, s) ≥ τ teacher>student  if  t ≥ τ > s weak / neither  otherwise gap = t − s

This is softened from the original hard 0.80 so near-misses are not mislabeled (see §18.2 Corrected gates).

19.5 Pattern-based cross-seed stability (the index-free fix)

The index-free measure of whether the same knowledge re-emerges under a new SAE seed. Probe set of M = 40,000 stored tokens. Encode the probe through the seed-0 SAE → A0 and the seed-1 SAE → A1, each [M × H], H = 16384. Z-score every column over the M tokens (→ ž0, ž1). For each seed-0 detector latent d of knowledge item C:

ρd = maxj   Pearson( A0[:,d], A1[:,j] ) = maxj 1M ∑m   ž0[m,d] · ž1[m,j] stability(C) = meand ρd   range 0..1; ~1 = a latent with the SAME firing pattern re-emerges under a new seed

19.6 Index-Jaccard stability (deprecated)

Deprecated — why it failsThe old measure was the Jaccard of the (layer, latent-INDEX) sets between seed 0 and seed 1. It is ≈0 by construction: two independently-trained SAEs learn permuted, different dictionaries — index k in seed 0 ≠ index k in seed 1 — so it measures index identity, not knowledge stability. Superseded by the pattern-based measure (§19.5).

19.7 Null control (shuffled-pair)

Per-side strength: tc = best-layer mean detector AUC for teacher knowledge item c; sc likewise for the student. The pair score must depend on which pair:

score(ct, cs) = max( 0,  min( tct − 0.5,   scs − 0.5 ) )   centered at chance 0.5; min throttles strong-teacher × weak-student real_mean = meanc ∈ shared score(c, c) for each of 100 random permutations π:   nullk = meanc score(c, π(c)) null_mean, null_std = mean / std over the 100  ;  gap = real_mean − null_mean  ;  z = gap / null_std

Pass = large positive gap with small null spread (high z).

19.8 Cross-layer routing (index-free, across depth)

Probe M tokens; encode at each captured layer L → AL [M × H]; z-score columns. Let dL = knowledge item C's top detector latent at layer L. The routing matrix and its summaries:

RC[L, L′] = Pearson( AL[:,dL],   AL′[:,dL′] ) over the M tokens   diagonal = 1 coherence(C) = mean of the OFF-diagonal entries of RC detector→any:   maxcorr(L → L′) = maxj Pearson( AL[:,dL], AL′[:,j] )   does C's layer-L pattern reappear ANYWHERE at L′?

19.9 Cross-reference

Two ingredients are derived earlier in this report and only linked here. The SAE itself is an OpenAI-style TopK SAE (k = 32, d_hidden = 16384, recon-MSE + AuxK; §9.3, §13). The distillation objective is the knowledge-activation-space loss that aligns teacher and student knowledge vectors (§10).

Magnitude, not significanceWith M = 40,000 probe tokens, correlation magnitudes are robust, but even tiny correlations come out "statistically significant" at that sample size — so interpret every correlation by its magnitude, not by significance.

21.Weighted Frequency Score (TraceRouter §3.1) — a second feature selector

An independent way to anchor SAE features to a knowledge item, adapted from TraceRouter (arXiv 2601.21900, §3.1). Where the detector-AUC (§19.2) is rank-based ("does this latent rank positives above negatives?"), the Weighted Frequency Score is magnitude-based ("does this latent fire harder / more often on sensitive than on non-sensitive prompts?"). Two different statistics that should converge if a feature genuinely encodes the knowledge — so WFS is run as a complement and cross-check to AUC, not a replacement.

21.1 Exact formulas

For SAE latent m, over a set of tokens (token-level, not example-pooled — see 21.2):

f(m) = P( am > 0 )   activation frequency — fraction of tokens where m fires μ(m) = E[ am  |  am > 0 ]   conditional mean magnitude — how hard it fires when it does WFS(m) = f(m) · μ(m)   computed once over SENS tokens, once over NON-SENS tokens ΔWFS(m) = WFSsens(m) − WFSnon-sens(m)   the selector; Top-k by ΔWFS = sensitive feature set

Sensitive = a knowledge item's positives; non-sensitive = its hard negatives. For non-negative TopK-SAE codes, f·μ is algebraically the plain mean activation — but we keep f and μ separate because the "how often" vs "how hard" split is the whole point, and lets us read why a feature is selected.

21.2 Why token-level (and not example-pooled)

Design decision — granularityWFS is a within-model selector, so the cross-family tokenizer-mismatch reason for example-pooling (§19.1) does not apply. If WFS is fed mean-pooled example vectors, almost every latent has pooled value > 0, so f ≈ 1 for nearly all features — the f/μ decomposition collapses and WFS degenerates to "mean pooled activation." We therefore compute it token-level, faithful to the paper. Empirically f then spans 0.20–0.95 (e.g. Hungarian latent #770 fires on 95% of Hungarian tokens, fnon-sens·μ ≈ 0.0003), confirming the decomposition is live.

ΔWFS rides alongside as the magnitude signal — at a fixed AUC-chosen layer it agrees on which features (C1), and its magnitude is what the knowledge-activation distillation loss (§10) weights by. WFS is also the source-feature selector for pathway tracing (§22). Artifacts: feature_scores/{model}_alllayer_wfs.parquet.

22.Causal Semantic Pathway Tracing (TraceRouter §3.2)

The routing of §20 is correlational (do detectors at different depths fire together?). This section is causal: identify the source neurons that carry a knowledge item, sever them, and measure what collapses downstream. Faithful to TraceRouter §3.2, adapted to text LMs.

22.1 Pipeline & the four design decisions

22.2 Feature Influence Score (Eq. 5)

For every downstream dense neuron m (layers l > l*), over the knowledge item's sensitive tokens, with am the clean and âm the post-intervention activation:

Δzero-out(m) = E[ | am − âm | ]   activation shift caused by severing S_src FIS(m) = f(m) · μ(m) · Δzero-out(m)   = WFSdense(m) × causal shift; Top-FIS/layer = the pathway

22.3 Factorial intervention (two sites × real/random)

To disentangle where the causal cut matters, we re-score the knowledge item's best downstream detector under a factorial of two intervention sites, each with a real and a magnitude-matched random option. Reported as absolute mean downstream AUC (lower = more collapse; clean = original):

  • onset l*: none / src (64 |Zproj|) / randS (64 random, magnitude-matched at l*)
  • downstream: none / path (Top-10 FIS / layer) / randD (10 random, magnitude-matched / layer)

The two clean reads: src < clean ⇒ the source is causally necessary (source-bottlenecked); src+path < src+randD ⇒ the downstream path is real (collapses beyond magnitude-matched random). The magnitude-matched random controls (the paper's own "random suppression") rule out "the drop is merely from removing high-variance neurons."

Why the random controls are essentialMeasuring a detector at a layer whose neurons were just zeroed is partially circular (you remove inputs the detector may read). Hence both a downstream control (src+randD) and an onset control (randS+path): an effect is only credited to selection when the real cut beats its magnitude-matched random twin.

Finding 2 — the cross-family difference is circuit architectureQwen has no source-bottlenecked knowledge at all: zeroing the onset source does nothing for any item (src ≈ clean), yet the per-layer path collapses safety/math/medical/language (real vs src+randD). Both Llama models, by contrast, localize safety/medical to a severable onset source. So the family difference is the shape of the circuit, and it is sharpest exactly on the safety/medical concepts: the teacher concentrates them at an onset source that the cross-family student simply does not have. This is the concrete reason teacher→Qwen transfer is hard — there is no single source set whose Qwen analogue can be aligned; the knowledge must be matched along the depth-distributed path (the cross-family/cross-depth correspondence problem, §5).
